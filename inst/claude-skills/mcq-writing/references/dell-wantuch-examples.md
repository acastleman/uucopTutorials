# Worked Flawed → Revised MCQ Pairs

Reproduced and annotated from Dell KA, Wantuch GA. *How-to-guide for writing multiple choice questions for the pharmacy instructor.* Currents in Pharmacy Teaching and Learning 2017; 9:137–144, Figures 2–4. Use these as templates when teaching MCQ revision, or as comparison cases when auditing existing items.

---

## Example 1 — Knowledge / Recall Level

**Topic:** Treatment of erosive esophagitis.

### Flawed version

> A patient is diagnosed with erosive esophagitis. Which of the following would be an appropriate treatment regimen and medication administration for this patient?
>
> a. Pantoprazole 40 mg po daily given 40 minutes before breakfast would be an appropriate regimen
> b. Ranitidine 150 mg po BID given with food would be an appropriate regimen
> c. Omeprazole 20 mg po daily given 60 minutes before breakfast would be an appropriate regimen
> d. Sucralfate 1 g po QID before meals and at bedtime would be an appropriate regimen

### Problems
1. **Two questions in one stem** — asks about both the regimen and the administration timing.
2. **Repetition in choices** — every option ends with "would be an appropriate regimen" (rule 2 — streamlining).
3. **Multiple correct answers** — pantoprazole (a) and omeprazole (c) are both correct PPI regimens for erosive esophagitis (rule 12).

### Revised version

> Which of the following is the most appropriate treatment for erosive esophagitis?
>
> a. Pantoprazole 40 mg po daily
> b. Ranitidine 150 mg po twice daily
> c. Sucralfate 1 g po four times daily

### What changed
- Stem reduced to one question and uses "most appropriate" to set the *best-answer* framing.
- Trailing repetition stripped from every choice.
- Omeprazole removed to leave a single key (pantoprazole).
- Choices are parallel: drug + dose + route + frequency, all spelled out (BID → twice daily; QID → four times daily) per Rule 4.

## Example 2 — Interpretation / Application Level

**Topic:** Antibiotic contraindication in an elderly patient with reduced renal function presenting with uncomplicated UTI.

### Flawed version

> __________ would not be an option in a 62 year old 5'4" female patient weighing 55 kg and SCr 1.3 mg/dL for treatment of a UTI who has NKDA.
>
> a. Bactrim
> b. Fluoroquinolones
> c. Nitrofurantoin
> d. Ceftriaxone

### Problems
1. **Blank at the start of the stem** — student cannot parse what is being asked until the end, then must scroll back (rule 5).
2. **Negative phrasing without emphasis** — "would not be an option" buried in the stem (rule 7).
3. **Specialty/uncommon abbreviations** (NKDA is borderline; if used, define) (rule 4).
4. **Non-parallel options** — (a) is a brand, (b) is a class, (c) and (d) are generics; mixing class and specific drugs in the same option list is a logical cue (rule 11). Also, not all fluoroquinolones are even indicated for UTI.

### Revised version

> A 62 year old, 5'4" female weighing 55 kg, with a serum creatinine of 1.3 mg/dL and no known drug allergies, presents to your clinic with an uncomplicated urinary tract infection. Which of the following treatment options is contraindicated for this patient?
>
> a. Bactrim
> b. Cipro
> c. Macrobid
> d. Rocephin

### What changed
- Stem rewritten as a question, with the blank moved to the end (now implicit in "Which of the following...").
- Negative phrasing replaced by "contraindicated" — a positively phrased question about a negative concept. Cleaner cognitive load.
- All abbreviations spelled out (UTI → urinary tract infection, NKDA → no known drug allergies).
- All four choices are now brand-name specific medications — parallel in kind. The discriminating concept (renal function precluding nitrofurantoin in this CrCl) is preserved.

## Example 3 — Problem Solving / Evaluation Level

**Topic:** JNC-8 hypertension recommendation in a patient with comorbid osteoarthritis.

### Flawed version

> A pleasant 62 year old African American male presents to your ambulatory care clinic for a follow up visit to see his trusted pharmacist. His past medical history includes hypertension and osteoarthritis. Today, you measured his blood pressure appropriately, after he has been sitting for 5 minutes, and you get 147/94 mm Hg. He states that this reading is consistent with the ones he's been getting when he goes to the grocery store. His home medication regimen includes amlodipine 5 mg po daily and hydrochlorothiazide 12.5 mg po daily. He states that he has been trying to follow your recommendations for a low salt diet but is having a hard time with this as his wife does all the cooking. According to JNC-8, which of the following is NOT an option for the treatment of his hypertension today?
>
> a. Add naproxen 250 mg po BID to his regimen
> b. Increase amlodipine to 10 mg po daily
> c. Increase hydrochlorothiazide to 25 mg po daily
> d. Add lisinopril 10 mg po daily to his regimen

### Problems
1. **Window dressing throughout the stem** — "pleasant", "trusted pharmacist", grocery-store BP readings, wife-does-the-cooking — none of it changes the JNC-8 answer (rule 8).
2. **Negative stem** — "NOT an option" (rule 7).
3. **Implausible distractor masquerading as a clinical decoy** — naproxen is not a hypertension drug; in a "which is NOT an option" frame this becomes trivially obvious. The combination of (1) + (3) is the classic test-wise giveaway: negative phrasing + implausible option = automatic key.
4. **Non-parallel verb pattern** — three options start with "Increase/Add", one with the same family — but the deeper non-parallelism is two "Add" options vs two "Increase" options, with the answer being the obvious mismatched one in a clinical sense.

### Revised version

> A 62 year old African American male, with hypertension and osteoarthritis presents to clinic for follow up. Current medications include amlodipine 5 mg po daily and hydrochlorothiazide 25 mg po daily. His blood pressure today is 147/94 mmHg and patient reports this is consistent with his home readings. According to JNC-8, what is the most appropriate recommendation to address this patient's hypertension?
>
> a. Increase amlodipine to 10 mg po daily
> b. Decrease hydrochlorothiazide to 12.5 mg po daily
> c. Discontinue amlodipine 5 mg po daily
> d. Initiate lisinopril 10 mg po daily

### What changed
- Case stem cut to load-bearing facts only.
- Negative phrasing replaced with positive "most appropriate."
- Implausible distractor (naproxen) removed; every option is now a real hypertension management move.
- Verbs deliberately varied across choices (*increase / decrease / discontinue / initiate*) so no single verb pattern is a cue and no choice becomes the outlier.

Authoring note from the paper: a common subtler flaw to avoid is three options using *increase* and one using *initiate* — the *initiate* option becomes a logical-cue outlier. Different starting verbs for every choice prevents that pattern.

## How to Use These Examples

- **Teaching tool** — show flawed and revised side-by-side to faculty learning MCQ construction.
- **Audit reference** — when reviewing a tutorial item that "feels off," compare to the closest pattern here to name the specific flaw.
- **Distractor brainstorming model** — Example 3 demonstrates how to vary verbs and pick clinically plausible distractors when revising. Apply the same logic when authoring fresh items.
